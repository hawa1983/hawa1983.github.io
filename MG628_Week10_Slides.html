FOUNDATIONS

What Is Time Series Data?

A time series is a sequence of data points collected at successive, equally-spaced points in time.

Everyday Examples

  • Monthly retail sales over 5 years
  • Weekly ER admission counts
  • Daily COVID-19 case reports by county
  • Annual municipal tax revenue
  • Hourly electricity consumption

⚙️ Key Requirement

Data must be at regular, equal intervals. Without this, time-based operations like rolling averages break down.

Daily Weekly Monthly Quarterly Annual
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

So what exactly is time series data? Simply put, it is any dataset where we have recorded the same measurement repeatedly over time — at regular, equal intervals. Think monthly sales figures, weekly hospital admissions, or daily case counts.

The key word here is equally spaced. We need consistent intervals to detect patterns. Our course dataset — sales_data.csv — uses monthly sales observations. This is the file you will use for both Activity 1 and Activity 2 this week.

FOUNDATIONS

The DNA of a Time Series: Four Components

T

Trend

The long-term upward or downward direction in the data.

S

Seasonality

Regular, repeating patterns (e.g., higher ER visits every winter).

C

Cyclical

Long-term oscillations (often economic) without a fixed period.

I

Irregular

Random "noise" or unpredictable black-swan events.

Data = Trend + Seasonality + Cyclical + Irregular  (Additive Model)
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Every time series you will ever encounter is made up of four building blocks — think of them as the DNA of your data. First, the Trend — is the overall direction going up, down, or flat over the long run? Second, Seasonality — are there patterns that repeat at the same time every year? Third, Cyclical movements — longer waves that follow economic or political cycles, without a fixed schedule. And fourth, Irregular variation — the unpredictable noise, the one-off events, the things no model can anticipate.

Our goal this week is to separate these components so we can see what is really driving the data.

TREND

Understanding Trend

  • The long-term, sustained direction of a series
  • Upward — consistent growth over years
  • Downward — systematic decline over time
  • Flat — stable, no meaningful direction
  • Can be linear or curved (non-linear)

📈 Upward Trend (illustrative)

Each period generally higher than the last = upward trend

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Trend is the big picture — if you zoom out and look at your data across years, is it drifting upward, downward, or holding steady? That drift is the trend.

Data bounces up and down from month to month — that is noise. A trend is what you see when you step back and squint. The 12-month moving average is the best visual tool to reveal the trend by smoothing out the monthly noise. In Activity 2, you will extract exactly this from your sales_data.csv file.

SEASONALITY

Seasonality: Patterns That Repeat

Key Traits

  • Repeats on a fixed, known period
  • Predictable once identified
  • Can be additive or multiplicative
  • Most actionable pattern for planning

Sector Examples

🛍️ Retail: Sales peak in November–December (holiday season)

🏥 Healthcare: ER admissions surge every winter (flu season)

🏛️ Government: Budget requests and tax revenue spike quarterly

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Seasonality is the pattern that comes back reliably, like clockwork. Retail sales spike every November and December — that is seasonal. ER visits peak every winter as flu season hits — seasonal. Tax filing deadlines cause workload spikes every April — seasonal.

What makes seasonality powerful is that it is predictable. Once you have identified the seasonal pattern, you can plan for it. You can staff up in advance. You can order inventory early. You can budget for the surge before it arrives. This is where forecasting directly drives operational decisions.

COMPONENTS

Cyclical Patterns & Irregular Variations

🔄 Cyclical Variation

  • Waves lasting 2–10+ years
  • Tied to economic or political cycles
  • No fixed, predictable period
  • Example: GDP contractions, housing cycles

⚡ Irregular / Random

  • Cannot be predicted or modeled
  • 'Black swan' events: pandemic, natural disaster
  • Creates residual after removing T, S, C
  • Example: COVID-19 impact on patient volumes
Goal: Model T + S + C accurately → what remains is the Irregular residual
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

The third component is cyclical variation — longer waves that follow economic or political cycles. Think of a recession: business slows down, revenues dip, and then they recover. That pattern might unfold over three to seven years. Unlike seasonality, cyclical patterns do not have a fixed schedule.

The fourth component is irregular variation — the true wild cards. The pandemic. An unexpected natural disaster. A one-time regulatory change. These cannot be modeled. What we do instead is model everything else well, and treat what remains as the irregular residual. That residual tells us how much of our data we still cannot explain.

FOUNDATIONS

Stationarity: A Critical Requirement

✅ Stationary Series

  • Constant mean over time
  • Constant variance
  • Required for ARIMA models

❌ Non-Stationary Series

  • Mean drifts upward or downward
  • Variance may grow over time
  • Must be transformed before ARIMA
Fix: Differencing — subtract each observation from the previous one. This removes the trend and makes most business series stationary.

df['diff1'] = df['Sales'].diff()
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Before we can apply advanced forecasting models like ARIMA, we need to talk about stationarity. A stationary time series is one where the statistical properties — the mean and variance — stay constant over time. It does not trend upward or downward, and it does not become more or less volatile as time passes.

Most real-world business data is non-stationary, and that is fine. We just need to transform it first. The most common technique is differencing — we subtract each observation from the previous one, which removes the trend and stabilizes the mean. We cover the formal test for this — the ADF test — on slide 22.

DECOMPOSITION

Additive vs. Multiplicative Models

FeatureAdditiveMultiplicative
FormulaY = T + S + C + IY = T × S × C × I
When to useSeasonal swings are constant in sizeSeasonal swings grow with the trend
ExampleSales vary by ±$10K every DecemberSales vary by ±10% every December
TransformationNone neededLog-transform to make additive
Rule of thumb: if seasonal peaks grow taller as the trend rises → use Multiplicative.
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Once we know that a time series has multiple components, we have to decide how those components combine. The Additive model says: the total value equals the sum of all components. Use this when the seasonal swings are constant in size — sales always go up by about ten thousand dollars in December, regardless of how high the trend is.

The Multiplicative model says the components multiply together. Use this when the seasonal swings grow as the trend grows — sales always spike by ten percent in December, so a bigger trend means a bigger absolute swing. When in doubt, plot your data. If the seasonal peaks and troughs are getting taller as the series grows, you need the multiplicative model.

TREND ANALYSIS

Moving Average: Smoothing the Noise

MA(k) = (Yt + Yt-1 + ... + Yt-k+1) / k
  • k = window size (use 12 for monthly data)
  • Each point = average of the surrounding k values
  • Removes short-term fluctuations to reveal the trend
  • Larger k = smoother line, but loses more data endpoints

🎯 Interpretation Guide

📈 Rising smoothed line → upward trend

➡️ Flat smoothed line → trend leveling off

📉 Falling smoothed line → downward trend

df['MA12'] = df['Sales'].rolling(12).mean()

df[['Sales', 'MA12']].plot(figsize=(12,5))
plt.title('Sales with 12-Month Moving Average')
plt.legend(['Original', '12-Month MA'])
plt.show()

Averaging exactly 12 consecutive months covers one full year. Every seasonal peak and trough gets included exactly once — so they cancel out. What remains is the pure underlying trend, free of seasonal distortion.

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

The Moving Average is our first tool for isolating the trend. Instead of plotting the raw sales for each month — which includes all that seasonal noise — we replace each data point with the average of the surrounding twelve months. That smooths out all the peaks and valleys that repeat each year, leaving us with just the underlying direction.

Why twelve? Because we have monthly data, and averaging twelve consecutive months covers exactly one full year — so every seasonal peak and trough gets averaged in exactly once, and they cancel each other out. TREND ANALYSIS

Linear Regression: Quantifying the Trend

Sales = β₀ + β₁ × Time + ε
  • β₀ = intercept (starting value when time = 0)
  • β₁ = slope (average change per time period)
  • ε = residual error (what the model doesn't explain)
  • If β₁ = 2.5 → sales grow by $2,500 per month on average
  • Positive slope = upward trend; Negative slope = downward trend
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
import numpy as np

df['time_idx'] = np.arange(len(df))
X = df[['time_idx']]
y = df['Sales']

model = LinearRegression().fit(X, y)
slope = model.coef_[0]
print(f"Slope: {{slope:.3f}} units/month")

df['trend_line'] = model.predict(X)
df[['Sales', 'trend_line']].plot()
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

The second trend technique is linear regression. Where the moving average shows you the trend visually by smoothing the line, regression gives you a precise number — the slope.

The slope tells you, on average, how much sales change for every one unit increase in time. If the slope is 2.5, that means sales grow by about two thousand five hundred dollars every single month, on average. That is a powerful, concrete statement for a manager to make to a leadership team. Activity 2 Question 3 asks you to report the slope and interpret it in plain English — not just as a bare number.

FORECASTING

Exponential Smoothing: Weight Recent Data More

St = α × Yt + (1 – α) × St-1
  • α (alpha) = smoothing parameter, 0 < α < 1
  • High α → reacts quickly to recent changes (volatile data)
  • Low α → stable, weights history more (smooth data)
  • More realistic than MA: recent data matters more than old data

Three Versions

📌 SES — Simple: no trend, no seasonality

📌 Holt — Double: captures trend only

📌 Holt-Winters — Triple: trend + seasonality (use this for our data!)

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

The moving average treats all twelve months in its window equally. But think about that — should sales from two years ago have the same influence on next month's forecast as last month's actual sales? Probably not.

Exponential smoothing assigns weights that decay exponentially as observations get older. The most recent observation gets the highest weight, and the influence fades as you go back in time. For your sales data, which has both trend and seasonality, you will use the Holt-Winters model — also called Triple Exponential Smoothing. It is purpose-built for data like ours.

FORECASTING

Holt-Winters in Python

from statsmodels.tsa.holtwinters import ExponentialSmoothing

# Split: reserve last 12 months for testing
train = df.iloc[:-12]
test  = df.iloc[-12:]

model = ExponentialSmoothing(
    train['Sales'],
    trend='add',
    seasonal='add',
    seasonal_periods=12
).fit()

forecast = model.forecast(12)

plt.plot(train['Sales'], label='Training')
plt.plot(test['Sales'],  label='Actual')
plt.plot(forecast,       label='Forecast')
plt.legend(); plt.show()
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Two things I want you to notice. First, we split the data into a training set and a test set. We train on everything except the last twelve months, then we forecast those twelve months and compare to what actually happened. This is how you evaluate a forecasting model — you test it on data it has never seen.

Second, notice seasonal_periods=12. This tells the model that one complete seasonal cycle is twelve months. The model then learns the seasonal pattern and the trend simultaneously, and combines them to project forward. This one function does a lot of heavy lifting.

ARIMA

ARIMA: The Industry Standard

ARIMA = AutoRegressive + Integrated + Moving Average
AR(p)

AutoRegressive

Uses p past values as predictors. Last month's sales help predict this month's.

I(d)

Integrated

Differences the series d times to achieve stationarity. d=1 removes a linear trend.

MA(q)

Moving Average

Uses q past forecast errors as predictors. The model learns from its own mistakes.

ARIMA(p,d,q) — choose parameters using ACF/PACF plots, or use auto_arima to search automatically
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

ARIMA is the industry-standard forecasting model. ARIMA stands for AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. The AR part uses past values of the series to predict the future — last month's sales help predict this month's. The I part applies differencing to make the series stationary. The MA part uses past forecast errors as predictors — it learns from its own mistakes.

These three components are controlled by three parameters: p for the AR order, d for how many times we difference, and q for the MA order. Choosing those parameters is the main skill — and we cover that on the next slide.

ARIMA

Choosing ARIMA Parameters (p, d, q)

p — AR Order

  • Check PACF plot
  • Count spikes before cutoff
  • PACF cuts at lag 2 → p=2

d — Differencing

  • Run ADF test first
  • Non-stationary → d=1
  • Rarely need d > 2

q — MA Order

  • Check ACF plot
  • Count spikes before cutoff
  • ACF cuts at lag 1 → q=1
💡 Quick start: Try ARIMA(1,1,1) as your baseline — one round of differencing, one AR lag, one MA correction. Reliable for most business time series.
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Selecting p, d, and q is where many students get stuck, so let me give you a practical workflow. Start with d: run the Augmented Dickey-Fuller test. If the series is non-stationary, set d equal to one. Then look at your PACF plot to choose p — count how many significant spikes appear before the plot cuts off to near zero. Do the same with the ACF plot for q.

If you just want to get started, try ARIMA(1,1,1) as your baseline. It handles simple trends and is a reasonable starting point for most business time series. Tune from there after seeing your accuracy metrics.

ARIMA

SARIMA: Adding the Seasonal Layer

SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q,m)

Non-Seasonal (p,d,q)

  • p — AR order (lags of the series)
  • d — Differencing order
  • q — MA order (lags of forecast errors)

Seasonal (P,D,Q,m)

  • P — Seasonal AR order
  • D — Seasonal differencing (usually 1)
  • Q — Seasonal MA order
  • m — Period (12 for monthly data)
For monthly sales with seasonality → start with SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,0,12)
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

When your data has both trend and seasonality, we extend ARIMA to SARIMA. SARIMA has a second set of parameters in parentheses that handle the seasonal component separately. The capital P, D, Q work exactly like their lowercase counterparts, but they operate at the seasonal lag — instead of looking at the previous month, they look at the same month last year.

For monthly data, m equals twelve. Start with a reasonable baseline like SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,0,12) and tune from there using AIC scores.

STATIONARITY

Testing for Stationarity: The ADF Test

from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller

result = adfuller(df['Sales'])
print(f"ADF Statistic: {{result[0]:.4f}}")
print(f"p-value:       {{result[1]:.4f}}")

if result[1] < 0.05:
    print("STATIONARY — ready for ARIMA.")
else:
    print("NON-STATIONARY — apply differencing and re-test.")

✅ p < 0.05 → Reject H₀ → Series is stationary → proceed to ARIMA

⚠️ p ≥ 0.05 → Fail to reject H₀ → Apply differencing, then re-test

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Before fitting any ARIMA model, always run the Augmented Dickey-Fuller test. The null hypothesis is that the series is non-stationary. If p < 0.05, we reject the null and conclude the series is stationary — proceed to ARIMA. If p ≥ 0.05, the series is non-stationary, and we need to difference it.

Difference the data, run the ADF test again, and repeat until the series is stationary. For most business data with a linear trend, one round of differencing — setting d equal to one — is usually enough.

EVALUATION

How Do We Know If Our Forecast Is Good?

MAE

Mean Absolute Error

avg(|Actual − Forecast|)

✓ Easy to interpret (same units)

✗ Treats all errors equally

RMSE

Root Mean Square Error

√avg((Actual − Forecast)²)

✓ Penalizes large errors more

✗ Sensitive to outliers

MAPE

Mean Absolute % Error

avg(|Error| / |Actual|) × 100

✓ Scale-free, comparable

✗ Undefined if actual = 0

Lower is better for all three. Use MAPE when comparing models across products or scales.
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Building a forecast is only half the job. The other half is knowing whether it is accurate enough to trust. MAE is the average absolute difference between our forecast and reality — easy to explain to a non-technical audience. RMSE squares the errors before averaging, which means large errors get penalized much more heavily. MAPE expresses accuracy as a percentage — great for comparing across different products or scales.

A MAPE below ten percent is generally considered excellent for business forecasting. But always interpret in context — for extremely volatile data, twenty percent might actually be quite good. When you report your model accuracy, always include the metric AND interpret what it means for the decisions being made.

EVALUATION

Calculating Forecast Accuracy in Python

import numpy as np

actual        = test['Sales'].values
forecast_vals = model.forecast(12).values

MAE  = np.mean(np.abs(actual - forecast_vals))
RMSE = np.sqrt(np.mean((actual - forecast_vals)**2))
MAPE = np.mean(np.abs((actual - forecast_vals) / actual)) * 100

print(f"MAE:  {{MAE:.2f}}")
print(f"RMSE: {{RMSE:.2f}}")
print(f"MAPE: {{MAPE:.2f}}%")
MAPE <10% = Excellent 10–20% = Good 20–50% = Acceptable >50% = Poor — revisit model
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Here is how you calculate all three metrics with just a few lines of NumPy. The key is that you compare your forecast values against the actual test values — the data you held out before fitting.

Remember: these benchmarks are rules of thumb. When you report your model accuracy, always say what the number means in context. 'MAPE of 7.3%' alone is meaningless to most managers. 'Our forecast is off by less than 8% on average' is something they can act on.

LIMITATIONS

Limitations of MA & Regression — Activity 2, Q5

Moving Average

  • Loses k-1 data points at the start of the series
  • Cannot forecast future values directly
  • Does not model seasonality
  • Reacts to trend changes with a lag

Linear Regression

  • Assumes straight-line trend (often wrong in reality)
  • Sensitive to outliers distorting the slope
  • Ignores autocorrelation between observations
  • Cannot capture seasonal patterns alone
These are exploratory tools — great for understanding data, but not the final forecasting step.
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Activity 2 Question 5 asks you to discuss limitations — so let me build your answer here. For the moving average: it is inherently backward-looking and cannot project into the future. It loses data at the beginning because the first window is not complete. And it has no awareness of seasonal patterns.

For regression: the assumption of a straight-line trend is often wrong. More critically, regression assumes that observations are independent of each other — which is almost never true for time series. Each month's sales are correlated with the previous month. Violating this assumption means our confidence intervals and hypothesis tests are unreliable. These are exploration and communication tools — they set the stage for the more powerful models we build next.

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

For the healthcare administrators — here is your scenario. ER admissions follow a strong seasonal pattern: they peak in winter due to flu and respiratory illness, and they drop in summer. The cost of over-staffing by ten percent is far lower than the cost of a dangerous nurse-to-patient ratio during a flu surge. This is where forecasting directly impacts patient safety — not just the budget.

TIPS

Common Mistakes to Avoid

✗ Forgetting parse_dates when loading CSV
✓ Always use parse_dates=['Date'], index_col='Date' — verify with df.index.dtype
✗ Fitting ARIMA on non-stationary data
✓ Run ADF test first; difference until stationary (p < 0.05), then fit ARIMA
✗ Evaluating accuracy on training data
✓ Always hold out a test set before fitting; evaluate only on the held-out test set
✗ Reporting 'MAPE = 8.3%' with no interpretation
✓ Say: 'MAPE of 8.3% = excellent accuracy — our forecast is off by less than 9% on average'
✗ Choosing the model with the best training fit
✓ Compare models on test-set performance only; lower out-of-sample error wins
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Let me share the five most common mistakes I see in student submissions. First: forgetting to parse dates — your plot will show numbers instead of dates, and nothing will work correctly. Second: fitting ARIMA before testing for stationarity — this invalidates your model entirely. Third: evaluating your model on the training data — that is like grading yourself on an open-book exam you wrote yourself.

Fourth: reporting MAPE as a bare number without saying what it means. Fifth: choosing your model based on training fit instead of test set performance. The goal is generalization, not memorization. A model that fits training data perfectly but fails on new data is worse than useless.

DIAGNOSTICS

Reading ACF and PACF Plots

ACF — Autocorrelation Function

  • Correlation of series with its own lags
  • Includes direct AND indirect effects
  • Seasonal: spikes at lags 12, 24, 36...
  • Use to choose q in ARIMA

PACF — Partial Autocorrelation

  • Direct correlation after removing indirect effects
  • Cuts off sharply for AR processes
  • More targeted than the ACF
  • Use to choose p in ARIMA
from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf, plot_pacf
plot_acf(df['Sales'], lags=36)
plot_pacf(df['Sales'], lags=36)
plt.show()
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

ACF and PACF plots are diagnostic tools that help you choose the right parameters for your ARIMA model. The ACF plot shows the correlation between the series and its own past values. If spikes appear at lags twelve and twenty-four for monthly data, that confirms a seasonal pattern. Use ACF to choose q.

The PACF plot shows the direct relationship between the series and each lag, after removing the influence of all shorter lags. Significant spikes in the PACF at lags one and two, followed by a sharp cutoff, suggests an AR(2) model. Use PACF to choose p.

STATIONARITY

Differencing: Making a Series Stationary

First Difference

Y't = Yt − Yt−1
df['diff1'] = df['Sales'].diff()

Seasonal Difference

Y't = Yt − Yt−12
df['sdiff'] = df['Sales'].diff(12)

What Differencing Does

  • Converts levels to changes — removes the trend
  • First diff (d=1) → removes linear trend
  • Seasonal diff (D=1) → removes annual seasonality
  • Re-run ADF after each diff — stop when p < 0.05
  • Over-differencing introduces noise — stop early!
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Differencing is the most important transformation in time series analysis. Instead of working with the raw sales values, we work with the month-to-month changes. This removes the trend — if sales grow by a constant amount each month, the differences will be roughly constant, and the drift disappears.

The critical rule: only difference as many times as necessary. Over-differencing creates artificial patterns. Run the ADF test after each round and stop as soon as the series is stationary. For most business data, one round of differencing is usually enough.

INTERPRETATION

Forecast Uncertainty: Confidence Intervals

No forecast is perfectly certain. A confidence interval shows the range where the true future value is likely to fall.
  • 95% CI: We expect the actual value to fall within this band 95% of the time
  • CIs widen as we forecast further into the future — more uncertainty over time
  • Narrow CI = model fits well; Wide CI = high uncertainty, use with caution
  • Always show CIs in presentations — it is intellectually honest
  • A point forecast without uncertainty bands can mislead decision-makers
forecast_obj = model.get_forecast(12)
summary = forecast_obj.summary_frame()
# Columns: mean, mean_ci_lower, mean_ci_upper
plt.plot(summary['mean'], label='Forecast')
plt.fill_between(summary.index,
    summary['mean_ci_lower'],
    summary['mean_ci_upper'],
    alpha=0.3, label='95% CI')
plt.legend()
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

One of the biggest mistakes junior analysts make is presenting a single-line forecast without any indication of uncertainty. Every forecast is a probability distribution, not a single point. The confidence interval shows the range within which we expect the actual value to land, ninety-five times out of one hundred.

Notice that as you forecast further into the future, the bands widen. This is correct and expected. When you present your forecast to a leadership team, always include these bands. A forecast that claims false precision will undermine your credibility the moment reality deviates from the single line you drew.

Which Forecasting Method Should I Use?

ScenarioRecommended MethodWhy
Need to see the trend visuallyMoving Average (12-month)Smooths noise without assumptions
Need to report trend rate to managementLinear RegressionProvides slope with statistical test
Data has clear seasonality + trendHolt-Winters (Triple ES)Purpose-built for exactly this case
Complex seasonal + autocorrelated dataSARIMAMost powerful for time series
Need a quick baseline forecastARIMA(1,1,1)Reliable, low-parameter starting point

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Here is your decision table. Use it every time you approach a new forecasting problem. Start with the simplest method that fits the scenario. If you just need to show a trend to a stakeholder, a twelve-month moving average is perfect. If your data has both trend and seasonality — which is true for sales_data.csv — Holt-Winters is your first choice.

The key principle is parsimony: use the simplest model that achieves acceptable accuracy. Complex models are harder to explain, harder to maintain, and more prone to overfitting.

import pandas as pd, numpy as np, matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statsmodels.tsa.seasonal import seasonal_decompose
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller
from statsmodels.tsa.holtwinters import ExponentialSmoothing
from statsmodels.tsa.arima.model import ARIMA

# 2. LOAD
df = pd.read_csv('sales_data.csv', parse_dates=['Date'], index_col='Date')

# 3. VISUALIZE
df['Sales'].plot(title='Monthly Sales'); plt.show()

# 4. MOVING AVERAGE
df['MA12'] = df['Sales'].rolling(12).mean()
df[['Sales','MA12']].plot(); plt.show()

# 5. ADF TEST
result = adfuller(df['Sales']); print(f"p-value: {{result[1]:.4f}}")

# 6. DECOMPOSE
seasonal_decompose(df['Sales'], model='additive', period=12).plot(); plt.show()

# 7. HOLT-WINTERS
train, test = df.iloc[:-12], df.iloc[-12:]
hw = ExponentialSmoothing(train['Sales'], trend='add', seasonal='add', seasonal_periods=12).fit()
hw.forecast(12).plot(); plt.show()

# 8. ARIMA(1,1,1)
arima = ARIMA(train['Sales'], order=(1,1,1)).fit()
arima.forecast(12)

Bonus: Auto-Select Parameters with pmdarima

# Install if needed: pip install pmdarima
from pmdarima import auto_arima

model = auto_arima(
    train['Sales'],
    seasonal=True,
    m=12,
    stepwise=True,
    information_criterion='aic',
    trace=True
)
print(model.summary())
forecast = model.predict(12)
  • auto_arima tests many (p,d,q)(P,D,Q) combinations and selects the model with the lowest AIC
  • AIC — Akaike Information Criterion: measures fit while penalizing complexity. Lower = better.
  • trace=True shows the search process — great for learning how the selection works
  • Use as a time-saver; understand the manual workflow first so you can interpret the output
🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

Once you understand how to manually select p, d, and q, you can use auto_arima from the pmdarima library to automate the search. It tests many combinations of parameters and selects the one with the best AIC score — the Akaike Information Criterion, which balances model fit against complexity.

I recommend using auto_arima to check your manual selection: if they agree, you have strong confidence in your choice. If they disagree, investigate why — that discrepancy is usually educational. But do not use auto_arima as a substitute for understanding the manual workflow.

# Ljung-Box test: are residuals white noise?
from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox
lb = acorr_ljungbox(arima_model.resid, lags=[12], return_df=True)
print(lb)  # p > 0.05 → residuals are white noise ✓

1 — Residual Plot

Should look like random noise — no patterns or trends

2 — Histogram

Residuals should be roughly bell-shaped (normal distribution)

3 — Q-Q Plot

Points should fall along the diagonal reference line

4 — Ljung-Box p > 0.05

Confirms residuals are not autocorrelated ✓

🎙 PRESENTER NOTES▼

A final quality check before you trust your forecast: residual diagnostics. After fitting your ARIMA model, examine the residuals — the differences between what the model predicted and what actually happened. Ideally, residuals should look like white noise: random, no patterns, roughly normally distributed, and not correlated with each other.

The built-in plot_diagnostics method gives you four diagnostic plots at once. The Ljung-Box test formally tests whether any autocorrelation remains in the residuals. A p-value above 0.05 means the residuals are statistically indistinguishable from white noise — your model has captured all the structure it can. If patterns remain, your model is misspecified and needs adjustment.
