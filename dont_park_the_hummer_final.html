Section 1

The Hummer on the Pavement: Incumbency as Underutilized Capacity

In the early 1990s, the U.S. military's High Mobility Multipurpose Wheeled Vehicle — the Humvee — was commercialized as the Hummer. Engineered for extreme terrain: desert, mountain, mud, combat. Yet the vast majority of civilian Hummers never left the pavement. They became what critics called "parking lot queens" — monuments of underutilization. Capable machines driven like sedans.

This is the central strategic risk facing the SLPP as it approaches 2028.

Sierra Leone's People's Party arrives at its third-term campaign with a governance machine of real, demonstrable capacity: a school enrollment revolution, macroeconomic stabilization from 56% inflation to single digits, the ACC-SL strengthened, ECOWAS commitments upheld, the Directorate of Science, Technology and Innovation launched. The vehicle has power. The question is whether the party will take it off-road — or campaign as a parking lot queen, circling familiar strongholds while 2028 demands something altogether more demanding.

Doctrine Principle 1

Incumbency is not a resting position. It is an engine. Engines left idling consume fuel without producing motion. The SLPP's 2028 mandate is not to coast — it is to redline.

1.1 The Global Evidence on Third-Term Politics

Third-term bids are the most pressure-tested moment in democratic incumbency. Globally since 1990, ruling parties seeking a third consecutive term have succeeded in approximately 56.5% of cases — falling to 48.3% in Sub-Saharan Africa and 41.7% in West Africa. In competitive multiparty democracies, the rate drops to 38.2%.

2024 was a historically brutal year for incumbents worldwide. In Botswana, the BDP — which had governed since independence — was swept out after 58 years. In Senegal and Ghana, ruling coalitions lost to oppositions that campaigned purely on delivery failures. In the United Kingdom and India, long-governing parties were punished despite economic growth, because the gap between policy intention and citizen experience had widened beyond tolerance.

Figure 1: Third-Term Succession Win Rates by Region (Post-1990 Multiparty Elections). Source: IDEA Electoral Database; Afrobarometer; author analysis.

The data are unambiguous: third-term campaigns are won not on record alone, but on the demonstrated capacity to convert institutional strength into tangible citizen experience. The SLPP cannot campaign on what it built. It must campaign on what citizens felt being built.

1.2 The Hummer Paradox: Capacity Without Reach

The chart below plots major African incumbent parties along two axes: governance capacity and delivery performance. Parties in the upper-right quadrant — high capacity, high delivery — are the winners. Parties stranded in the high-capacity, low-delivery quadrant are the cautionary tales: the Hummer on the pavement.

Figure 2: The Hummer Paradox — Capacity vs. Delivery Scores, African Incumbents 2019–2024. Source: Ibrahim Index of African Governance; Afrobarometer; World Bank; author composite scoring.

The SLPP's current position — solid on capacity, lagging on delivery score relative to that capacity — is not a condemnation. It is a diagnosis. And diagnoses prescribe action. The 2028 doctrine is the treatment plan.

Section 2

Excel, Don't Stall: The Anti-Corinthian Imperative

In organizational behavior, the knowing-doing gap is the systematic failure of institutions that can articulate what needs to be done but cannot execute at scale. The party has excelled in policy design, institutional architecture, and macroeconomic framework. The third-term mandate requires it to excel in the harder, quieter work of last-mile delivery — the road that reaches the village, the clinic that stocks medicine, the school that has a teacher in the classroom on Monday morning.

Doctrine Principle 2

Excellence in announcement without excellence in delivery is the fastest path to a third-term defeat. The voter does not grade the policy paper. She grades her child's classroom.

2.1 The Knowing–Doing Gap: A West African Comparison

Where the gap between policy intention and delivery outcome is widest — Ghana's NPP and Nigeria's APC — the ruling party lost. Where it is narrowest — Senegal's APR in 2019 — incumbents held.

Figure 3: The Knowing-Doing Gap — Policy Intention vs. Delivery Outcome Scores, West African Incumbents. Source: Afrobarometer Round 10; World Bank Governance Indicators; author composite index.

Every district where Free Quality Education enrollment grew but teacher quality stagnated is a knowing-doing gap. Every community where roads were graded once and not maintained is a knowing-doing gap. Every SLEITI report showing $1.07 billion in uncaptured mineral revenue is a knowing-doing gap at industrial scale.

2.2 The Enrollment Achievement: What Closing the Gap Looks Like

School enrollment grew from approximately 2.0 million in 2018 to 3.4 million in 2024 — a 70% increase in six years with no precedent in Sierra Leone's post-independence history. This is the model the party must replicate across health, infrastructure, and economic inclusion.

Figure 4: Sierra Leone School Enrollment Growth (2015–2024). Source: Statistics Sierra Leone; Ministry of Basic and Senior Secondary Education; World Bank EdStats.

The 2028 campaign must lead with this data — not as a retrospective exhibit, but as a performance baseline. The question for voters is not: did enrollment grow? The question is: will quality match quantity? That is the knowing-doing gap that remains.

Section 3

Deliver Unconditionally: The Macedonian Standard

Governing parties that deliver only when conditions are optimal govern for themselves. Governing parties that maintain delivery cadence through fiscal pressure, global shocks, and internal stress govern for their people — and people remember.

The SLPP has governed through COVID-19's fiscal devastation, a global inflation shock peaking at 46–56% in 2022–2023, sustained currency depreciation, and a commodity export price cycle exposing structural revenue fragility. Through it all, the schools stayed open.

Doctrine Principle 3

Conditional delivery is electoral fragility. The 2028 mandate is to demonstrate that SLPP governance is a steady variable — not a function of favorable conditions. Deliver unconditionally, and let the data speak.

3.1 The Mineral Revenue Gap: The Biggest Opportunity

Sierra Leone exported approximately $1.12 billion in mineral commodities annually (2022–2023 SLEITI data). Government revenue capture: approximately $49.4 million — a capture rate of just 4.4%. The remaining $1.07 billion left the country.

Figure 5: Sierra Leone Mineral Revenue Capture Gap (SLEITI, 2022–2023 Average). Source: SLEITI Annual Report; Ministry of Mines and Mineral Resources.

A credible 2028 campaign commits to a specific and measurable mineral revenue capture target — not a percentage aspiration, but a number, a mechanism, and a deadline. Every point of additional capture is a resource that closes the knowing-doing gap without additional borrowing.

Section 4

Start Where You Are: The Precision Deployment Doctrine

Elections are won at the margins — in the districts where undecided voters live, in the communities where SLPP policy achievement is least known, in the chiefdoms where the Hummer has not yet left the pavement. Stronghold defense is a holding operation. Precision deployment of governance capacity is the off-road strategy that secures the historic third term.

Doctrine Principle 4

The SLPP does not need to win everywhere. It needs to win where it matters. Go where the Hummer hasn't been.

4.1 Kono District as the Electoral Barometer

Since 2012, Kono District has been the most reliable single-district swing indicator of Sierra Leone's national election outcome. In 2023, the SLPP retained Kono with a reduced majority — a signal the party cannot ignore heading into 2028.

Figure 6: Kono District as National Swing Indicator — SLPP vs APC Vote Share (2007–2023). Source: National Electoral Commission of Sierra Leone; author analysis.

The precision deployment doctrine assigns Kono specific and measurable delivery commitments: road access to mining communities, health facility upgrades, youth employment through local mineral processing, and transparent benefit-sharing from the district's extractive base.

4.2 The ECSL Trust Deficit

Afrobarometer Round 10 (April 2025) records a roughly 30-point decline in ECSL trust since 2018. Pre-2028 investments in electoral commission independence and voter registration transparency are not concessions to the opposition — they are insurance for the SLPP's own victory legitimacy. Win clean. Win convincingly.

Section 5

The Off-Road Strategy: Doctrine Summary & 2028 Imperatives

Principle 01
Deploy the Capacity

The SLPP's institutional architecture — PDU, NOC, Office of the Chief Minister, Monthly Cabinet Performance Review — exists to be driven. Activate every mechanism. Grade every output. Publish every metric.

Principle 02
Close the Knowing-Doing Gap

For every announced initiative, identify a measurable citizen-felt outcome. Enrollment grew — now quality must grow. Institutions strengthened — now citizen trust must strengthen.

Principle 03
Deliver Unconditionally

The $1.07B mineral revenue gap is the single most addressable unconditional delivery opportunity. A credible capture-rate commitment, with mechanism and timeline, is worth more than any campaign promise.

Principle 04
Go Where the Hummer Hasn't Been

Precision deployment means Kono, means underserved chiefdoms, means female youth voters, means first-time voters who were 13 years old when Bio won in 2018. Win the village. Win the republic.

Principle 05
Win Clean

The legitimacy premium of a clean landslide compounds into a governing mandate that the first two terms never fully had. Invest in electoral confidence infrastructure before polling day.

A Closing Argument for the Strategy Room

No Sierra Leonean ruling party has succeeded itself after two consecutive terms since 1996. The third-term bid in 2028 will be, in the truest sense, historically unprecedented in the modern democratic era.

The data say it is achievable. The comparative evidence says it requires something the SLPP has never had to prove before: that it can govern not just effectively in crisis, but excellently in expectation. That it can move from surviving the storm to building in the sunshine. That it can take the machine it has built — formidable, documented, real — off the pavement and into the terrain where the 2028 election will actually be decided: the village, the chiefdom, the household still waiting for the road.

Don't park the Hummer. The election is off-road.
